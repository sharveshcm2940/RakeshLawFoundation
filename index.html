Our Foundation

A Legacy of
Legal Excellence

Founded on 1 May 2022 by Senior Advocate N.R. Elango in memory of his son Rakesh, the Rakesh Law Foundation stands as a beacon of legal education and constitutional awareness in Chennai.

Inaugurated in the presence of Chief Minister M.K. Stalin, the foundation operates at the intersection of legal academia, public policy, and civic engagement — bringing together the judiciary, bar, and academia under one roof. Registered as a charitable trust, it maintains tax-exempt status under Section 12AB of the Income Tax Act.

Our Mission

"To promote justice and equity through legal education, constitutional scholarship, and community awareness — honouring the memory of Rakesh."

From flagship endowment lectures featuring Supreme Court judges to moot court competitions conducted in Tamil, the foundation nurtures the complete legal mind — equally versed in scholarship and humanity.

4th Endowment Lecture 19 April 2025
04

75-Year Journey of the Constitution of India

Justice Jasti Chelameswar 19 Apr 2025

Former Judge, Supreme Court of India. Commemoration of the 75th anniversary of the Indian Constitution — exploring the evolution of constitutional values and the judiciary's role in maintaining equity over seven decades.

This landmark 4th edition marked the 75th anniversary of the Constitution of India. Justice Chelameswar traced the arc of constitutional values from the founding moment to the present day, examining how the judiciary has upheld — and at times strained — the promises of the republic. The discussion centred on federalism, judicial independence, and the enduring relevance of the Preamble's ideals of justice, liberty, equality, and fraternity. Held in collaboration with RMRL and attended by legal scholars from across Chennai.

3rd Endowment Lecture 28 April 2024
03

Guilty Till Proved Innocent: Dark Areas of Criminal Jurisprudence

Dr. S. Muralidhar 28 Apr 2024

Former Chief Justice, Orissa High Court. An unflinching examination of structural flaws in criminal law where the presumption of innocence is compromised.

Guests: Justice Anita Sumanth · Justice N. Anand Venkatesh (Madras HC)

Dr. Muralidhar delivered a forensic examination of the "dark areas" of criminal law — spaces where the presumption of innocence is routinely eroded by systemic pressures. He identified bail denial, prolonged undertrial detention, and prosecutorial overreach as critical fault lines. The judge urged the public to be informed and stand up to injustice, and emphasised that judicial independence must be absolute even at the magistrate level to prevent wrongful prosecutions. The lecture was attended by sitting Madras High Court judges and drew significant engagement from the Chennai legal community.

2nd Endowment Lecture 16 April 2023
02

Pillars of Indian Democracy — Federalism Under Strain

Abhishek Manu Singhvi 16 Apr 2023

Senior Advocate & Member of Parliament. A comprehensive analysis of Indian federalism across constitutional, economic, and political fronts.

Singhvi delivered a wide-ranging lecture on the triple strain on Indian federalism — constitutional, economic, and political. He notably coined the phrase that the Centre had transformed "cooperative federalism" into "combative federalism," resulting in unsavoury confrontations between the Union and state governments. The lecture examined Article 356 misuse, financial devolution failures, and the erosion of state legislative autonomy. Singhvi concluded with a call for a renewed constitutional compact between the Centre and the states, grounded in the federal spirit of the Constitution's framers.

1st Endowment Lecture 16 April 2022
01

Justice and Equity — The Constitutional Role of the Governor

Kapil Sibal 16 Apr 2022

Senior Advocate & Member of Parliament. The inaugural lecture examined the Governor's office as a colonial legacy and argued for strict constitutional limits on its powers.

Guests: CM M.K. Stalin · N. Ram (The Hindu Group) · Udhayanidhi Stalin

The inaugural lecture of the foundation was a landmark occasion, attended by Chief Minister M.K. Stalin, N. Ram of The Hindu Group, and Udhayanidhi Stalin. Kapil Sibal argued that the office of Governor is a vestigial colonial institution with strictly limited constitutional mandate. He contended that a Governor should refer Bills to the President only when they conflict with existing parliamentary legislation — not as a political instrument to stall elected state governments. Sibal's lecture sparked significant national debate on gubernatorial overreach, coming at a time of escalating Centre-state tensions.
